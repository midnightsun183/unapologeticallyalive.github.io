A poem for our times

5/31/2020

1 Comment

 

And Mary said, My soul doth magnify the Lord, And my spirit hath rejoiced in God my Saviour. For he hath regarded the low estate of his handmaiden: for, behold, from henceforth all generations shall call me blessed. For he that is mighty hath done to me great things; and holy is his name. And his mercy is on them that fear him from generation to generation. He hath shewed strength with his arm; he hath scattered the proud in the imagination of their hearts. He hath put down the mighty from their seats, and exalted them of low degree. He hath filled the hungry with good things; and the rich he hath sent empty away. He hath helped his servant Israel, in remembrance of his mercy; As he spake to our fathers, to Abraham, and to his seed for ever.

​Luke 1:46-55

The Magnificat is a poem for our times, but not in the way it is often recited and performed. 

Usually sung in dulcet tones, controlled and perfect, the Magnificat is a frequent part of liturgical services in any number of Christian denominations. But in interacting with it in this way, have we missed the point? Do the sweet notes and sanitized environment in which we experience this poem mask its true power? 

My soul doth magnify the Lord, And my spirit hath rejoiced in God my Saviour

Ok, sounds nice enough. 

For he that is mighty hath done to me great things; and holy is his name.

Alright, more recognition of God’s greatness and some praise. Good stuff. Pretty par for the course in any liturgy. 

... he hath scattered the proud in the imagination of their hearts. He hath put down the mighty from their seats, and exalted them of low degree. He hath filled the hungry with good things; and the rich he hath sent empty away.

Hold up. Stop. Read that again. 

The mighty will be put down. 

The lowly raised up. 

The hungry filled. 

The rich sent away empty. 

This poem is not some rosey, innocent spiritual reflection sung by a happy-go-lucky young girl with a perfect first century life. 

This poem is the revolutionary declaration of change, the courageous shout of a young woman of color who finds herself pregnant, out of wedlock, in a society that will not forgive such a transgression, and who proclaims the power and justice of the Lord to those who have been oppressed by the powers and principalities of this world. 

I am often frustrated when I hear the magnificat sung or recited as part of the liturgy, not because it is not important, but because it is SO important. SO powerful. And the manner in which it is most often performed dilutes this power. 

This morning I heard it read as the closing to a sermon on Pentecost, on the indwelling, and the power, and the movement of the Spirit. On the ongoing fight for racial justice and equality in America. On the mighty being put down. On the lowly being raised up. On the hungry being filled and on the rich being sent away empty. 

I’m not sure if the Magnificat is for every Sunday, or if it is for every liturgy. I’m not sure if there is a time for it to be sung sweetly and recited with the sort of happiness that seems to pervade its essence in most of its performances. I think that these recitations and performances feed our complacency, feed our comfort with words that for many of us should stir discomfort. I think they also feed our comfortable image of Mary as some sweet, innocent girl who was compliant. Who did what she was told. Who submitted. 

And I believe that nothing could be further from the truth. 

To me, the Magnificat feels more raw than that. It feels less polished, less sanitized. It feels like the kind of declaration, the kind of prophecy that SHOULD make us uncomfortable, because change on the scale and of the sort that it describes is NEVER comfortable. It is NEVER easy. And it NEVER takes us “back to normal.” 

It is hard. 

It hurts. 

It is messy. 

It can feel out of control. 

As Mary would experience not long after declaring this prophecy, the birthing of something new and good and life-giving into the world is all of these things. And it is also worth it. 

The Magnificat is a poem for our times. But only if we can really hear it.

On the Pain of Knowing

2/17/2020

0 Comments

 
Ignorance is bliss. That’s what they say, right? I used to think that a lie. I love knowing things. How to speak another language. How to drive a tractor. How to roast a chicken. How the biology of a cell works. 

But those were simple things, and I was naive. Now I understand that knowing comes with a price tag, but it’s hidden. It’s a sleek salesman that hooks you on the high of the drug of knowledge before you can learn about the side effects. About the damaged relationships, the loss of sleep, the panic attacks, and the loneliness. Most of all the loneliness. 

The high of knowing wears off quickly, the self-satisfaction and pride in your knowledge a fleeting sensation. All you are left with is a view of the world without the rose colored glasses, and the conflicting desire to both protect those who still wear them, and to rip them from their eyes as well. 

Those other frameless few are of little comfort. Most of them are as disillusioned and heartbroken as you are. They have no energy left to comfort you. They cannot even comfort themselves. The community that you once enjoyed with the bespectacled masses is impossible when your vision has been corrected. You cannot unsee what you have seen. You never read the fine print of knowing.

A Different Kind of Christmas Eve

1/9/2018

0 Comments

 
A Different Kind of Christmas Eve

This Christmas Eve was the first time that I ever attended a midnight mass. This may come as a surprise to those not as familiar with the norms of many American Protestant denominations, but while I grew up going to a Christmas Eve service every year, it was not a mass, and it was not at midnight. I attended a regular church service (no communion) around 7PM, because in rural Pennsylvania no one is staying up until midnight for church. We’re going to sleep because there are probably animals to be fed early in the morning, and we are nothing if not practical.

This year was different. After several years of tolerating Christmas Eve services at the church where I grew up and being repeatedly upset or disappointed by the sermon or the comments from fellow congregants, I decided that I would no longer attend the service there and instead go to the midnight mass at the Episcopal church I currently attend. It was the best decision I made for my faith this Christmas. Let me explain.

The day before Christmas Eve I attended a family gathering during which I listened to my uncles bemoan how “they” are trying to remove the Confederate monuments and “rewrite history” to push “their” agenda. I sat there with a plastic smile plastered over my face and said nothing. I have mixed feelings about that decision ranging from, “You’ve got to pick your battles, and this is one you’re never going to win,” to “Come on, Emily. Someone has to stand up and be the voice of reason here.” Not being able to deal with it any longer and having a long drive ahead of me, I chose that moment to start making my goodbyes.

After that came the seemingly inevitable thing that I think most of us probably do- ruminate over what we woulda, coulda, shoulda said. I am terrible about this. I can get caught in a cycle of anger and self-righteousness that is neither productive nor emmulative of the character and mind of Christ. I often need reminders and literal timeouts to break this cycle and be reminded of the goodness of my fellow Christians and my own need for grace. Hence, the importance of the midnight mass the next evening.

Unlike the Christmas Eve services that I had been to previously, this service was a feast for the senses, and there was no emotional pressure to make a decision for Jesus near the end. There was no one wanting you to decide, on the spot and without first discussing it with any member of the clergy, if you wanted to “get saved”. There was dignity and grace and beauty. There was the smell of incense and the flickering of the candles. There was the glorious sound of the choir and orchestra that had practiced for ages to make every note a worthy sacrifice unto our Lord. There was the action of kneeling in the presence of God and physically crossing ourselves with prayer. But most importantly, there was the Eucharist. There was a physical communion with God and all the saints. There was a moment in which I was the recipient of boundless and undeserved grace and was reminded of my responsibility to show that grace to others, especially those that I feel do not deserve it.

If this were an easy task, I probably would have mastered it by now. Most of the world probably would have. But it’s not. It is the single most difficult thing a person can ever do. It is why I need these holy reminders. These reminders that there are people all over this country and this earth that really desire a more just and perfect world. These reminders that there are others working to make true that line of hope, “Your Kingdom come on earth, as it is in Heaven.” These reminders that I have a role to play in making that happen as well. And these reminders that when I screw it all up- and I will, again and again- there is cup overflowing with grace and mercy from which I am always invited to partake.

Family Reunions, Homelessness, and Getting Squared Away with the Local Rabbi

6/17/2017

0 Comments

 
“Are there many homeless people up around here?”
 
“A few. There are more and more it seems. The city’s moving up here.”
 
“There are so many down there. I see them at all the intersections. One of them holding up a sign saying ‘In the name of Jesus, help me. Out of work’" she scoffs. “And they won’t even come to church. My nephew has been talking to one of them, trying to get him to come to church. He gave him some money once and then watched him go around the corner and buy alcohol with it.”
 
“I don’t give money to anyone,” she says, shaking her head and frowning. 

​​
When he entered Capernaum, a centurion came to him, appealing to him and saying, “Lord, my servant is lying at home paralyzed, in terrible distress.” And he said to him, “I will come and cure him.”

    ______________________________________________________________________________

A leper came to him begging him, and kneeling he said to him, “If you choose, you can make me clean.” Moved with pity, Jesus stretched out his hand and touched him, and said to him, “I do choose. Be made clean!”

    _______________________________________________________________________________
 
Now when Jesus heard this, he withdrew from there in a boat to a deserted place by himself. But when the crowds heard it, they followed him on foot from the towns. When he went ashore, he saw a great crowd; and he had compassion for them and cured their sick. When it was evening, the disciples came to him and said, “This is a deserted place, and the hour is now late; send the crowds away so that they may go into the villages and buy food for themselves.” Jesus said to them, “They need not go away; you give them something to eat.” They replied, “We have nothing here but five loaves and two fish.” And he said, “Bring them here to me.” Then he ordered the crowds to sit down on the grass. Taking the five loaves and the two fish, he looked up to heaven, and blessed and broke the loaves, and gave them to the disciples, and the disciples gave them to the crowds. And all ate and were filled…

   _______________________________________________________________________________

Do you remember that time that someone came to Jesus for food or healing and he said, “You know, why don’t we go to the synagogue and get you squared away with the local rabbi?. Once you’re a regular attender, have cleaned yourself up some, and kicked this alcohol problem, we’ll talk about your job and housing issues.”
 
Does that story ring a bell for you? No? Me neither.
 
Maybe that’s because Jesus repeatedly met people where they were and dealt with their most pressing problems in that moment. He didn’t worry about the fact that the centurion was Roman and part of the imperial force that was oppressing his people. He didn’t let the uncleanness of the leper stop him from touching him making him well. He didn’t even turn away the crowds who had followed him in search of healing, even though he had been seeking quiet and solitude. And he most certainly did not drag them down to the local rabbi to get them plugged into the appropriate Temple Torah Study class before he met their most pressing needs. He also didn’t shake his head at them and scoff, frowning at their hope, nay their audacity, in approaching him for help.  
 
If we are to be the hands and feet of Christ in the world, we need to get with the program, and that frequently means pitching the program out the window, getting out onto the streets, learning about people’s needs and problems, and meeting them. Am I suggesting that we hand out $100 bills to every person begging for cash? No. I am suggesting that we shift our attitudes and our perspectives so that they are cultivated by the mercy and compassion of the God we claim to serve. I am suggesting that we do not know the pain and the circumstances that drive a person to spend any money that they have on alcohol to numb that pain, and we aren’t going to figure it out by frowning at them and walking away, feeling self-righteous in our secure knowledge that our money didn’t go towards the purchase of “the devil’s drink.”

   _______________________________________________________________________________

The opening lines were a conversation that I overheard between two self-claiming born-again Christians at my family reunion last weekend. I sat there listening but didn’t say anything. I don’t see the one very often, and I barely know the other. I didn’t want to raise a fuss. I didn’t want to create a scene. I didn’t want to cause trouble.

   _____________________________________________________________________________

“Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you tithe mint, dill, and cummin, and have neglected the weightier matters of the law: justice and mercy and faith. It is these you ought to have practiced without neglecting the others…”
 
   ______________________________________________________________________________
 
As much as I may criticize my relatives for their attitude toward the poor and the downtrodden, I must also hold myself accountable for my unwillingness to rock the boat, to stand up as Jesus would have and called them out for their callousness and lack of compassion. I have long held that a sin of omission is just as great as one of commission, and today my sin was just that- omitting my critique. Remaining silent. Allowing everyone to be comfortable, instead of calling them to be more genuine followers of Christ.

“Are there many homeless people up around here?”
 
“A few. There are more and more it seems. The city’s moving up here.”
 
“If the city is moving up here, maybe it’s time that the church moved onto the streets. Maybe it’s time we got with the program by pitching the programming and meeting God’s children where they are. Maybe it’s time we stand up, leave our comfortable pews, take up our crosses, and really FOLLOW our savior. Maybe it’s time we really were his hands and feet in the world today.”

ï»¿On Slowing Down

1/16/2017

0 Comments

 
This post was originally written in July, 2016 while while Emily was traveling in Segovia, Spain.
She walked slowly. That’s all. She walked slowly.

If you have ever had the occasion to walk anywhere with me, you know that I walk quickly. I do not dawdle. About the only time I ever ramble is if I am walking and eating gelato at the same time. 

​Otherwise, walking anywhere with me is like a race. My husband says that I walk quickly, expecting everyone to keep up, and then when they do try to keep pace, I walk even more quickly, until I am almost running. It drives him crazy.

​I also walk a lot, especially since coming to Spain in January. Not having a car, walking is a matter of necessity, but it is also something that I enjoy. I love exploring places on foot. I hate trying to see a new city in a car. I feel like I miss so much. I am impatient. I want to see and do everything.
She walked slowly. That’s all. She walked slowly.

​​Over the last month I have been travelling in a group. This brings its own set of challenges and rewards. You don’t have to eat every meal alone, there is someone to keep you company when you are lonely, and you can split costs, so you can do more stuff. You also can never seem to eat a meal alone, there is never any time to yourself, and despite things being cheaper if done in a group, getting everyone to agree on something to do wastes a lot of time. It has been a learning experience.

When I first met everyone, I had an idea of who I thought I would click with, and who I wouldn’t. I thought I would get along with the guy I already knew, and I wouldn’t get along with the woman who seemed really high maintenance and liked to stop and look in every shop or cafe.  

And she walked slowly.
A week or so into our trip, I found myself sitting on the floor of her room while she showed me all the things she had bought when she was out shopping that day. That’s another thing. She shopped a lot. I like shopping alright, but I was on a budget, so it wasn’t really on my to-do list.

​But I like to live vicariously, so I sat there and looked through all of her purchases. Before I knew it we were going on for an hour, chatting about faith, life, relationships, therapy… We connected. We shared our stories. We started doing stuff together, and encouraging each other to go do stuff on our own when we needed time away from the group.

During the last week of our trip, we had another really personal chat about some mental health issues that we both deal with and how we cope- for better or worse. She has experienced a lot of loss over the last year, and I was honored that she felt safe sharing her story with me. Some of the best moments of this trip, the ones that I will remember, were the conversations that I shared with her.

She walked slowly. I slowed down. We walked together for a while. I was humbled by her company, and I know that I am better for it.
